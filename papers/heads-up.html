Heads-up! Unsupervised Constituency Parsing via Self-Attention Heads

AACL-IJCNLP 2020
Bowen Li, Taeuk Kim, Reinald Kim Amplayo, Frank Keller

One-Line Summary

Unsupervised constituency parsing using self-attention heads

Background

Constituency parsing — decomposing a sentence into nested sub-phrases — traditionally requires expensive treebank annotations. Unsupervised parsing aims to recover this hierarchical structure without labeled data. Meanwhile, pre-trained transformers such as BERT have been shown to encode surprisingly rich syntactic information in their internal representations. A natural question arises: can the self-attention patterns of these models be directly leveraged to induce constituency trees, bypassing the need for explicit syntactic supervision altogether?

Method

  • Attention-based span scores: The method computes a syntactic affinity score for every possible text span by aggregating attention weights from selected self-attention heads in a pre-trained transformer. High scores indicate that the tokens within a span attend to each other more than to tokens outside, suggesting a coherent constituent.
  • Head selection: Not all attention heads encode syntax equally. The paper introduces a head selection strategy that identifies the most syntactically informative heads, filtering out heads that capture positional or semantic (rather than structural) patterns.
  • Greedy tree construction: Given the span scores, a greedy top-down algorithm recursively splits sentences into constituents, producing a full binary constituency tree without any labeled training data.

Results

  • Competitive unsupervised parsing: The approach achieves competitive F1 scores on the Penn Treebank (PTB) benchmark compared to existing unsupervised parsers that rely on more complex generative models or specialized training objectives.
  • Head-level analysis: Detailed analysis reveals that a small subset of attention heads in middle layers of BERT consistently encode syntactic constituency information, while other heads focus on different linguistic phenomena.
  • Robustness: The method generalizes across different pre-trained models and demonstrates stable performance without task-specific fine-tuning.

Why It Matters

This work provides concrete evidence that transformer self-attention heads implicitly learn hierarchical syntactic structure during pre-training. By showing that constituency trees can be extracted from attention patterns alone, it opens a lightweight, annotation-free pathway to syntactic analysis. The findings also deepen our understanding of what linguistic knowledge is encoded in different components of pre-trained language models, informing future model design and interpretability research.
