Universal Domain Adaptation for Robust Handling of Distributional Shifts in NLP

EMNLP 2023 Findings
Hyuhng Joon Kim, Hyunsoo Cho, Sang-Woo Lee, Junyeob Kim, Choonghyun Park, Sang-goo Lee, Kang Min Yoo, Taeuk Kim

One-Line Summary

A universal domain adaptation framework that jointly handles covariate shift and label shift in NLP by learning domain-invariant representations and detecting out-of-domain samples.

Background

Domain adaptation in NLP typically assumes that the source and target domains share the same label space, only differing in input distribution (covariate shift). In practice, however, the target domain may contain novel classes absent from the source (label shift), or some source classes may be irrelevant to the target. Universal domain adaptation (UniDA) tackles the most general setting where the label spaces can overlap partially, requiring the model to (1) classify shared-class samples correctly and (2) detect target-private samples as unknown. Prior UniDA work has largely focused on vision; adapting these ideas to NLP, where representations and domain gaps behave differently, remains underexplored.

Method

  • Domain-invariant representation learning. The model learns representations that are aligned across source and target domains so that shared-class samples cluster together regardless of domain origin.
  • Sample-level transferability scoring. A calibrated scoring mechanism estimates how likely each target sample belongs to a shared class versus a target-private (unknown) class, enabling selective adaptation.
  • Unknown-class detection. Samples with low transferability scores are flagged as out-of-distribution, preventing negative transfer from classes that have no counterpart in the source domain.
  • End-to-end training. Representation alignment, transferability scoring, and classification are optimized jointly, avoiding error propagation from separate pipeline stages.

Results

  • Achieves consistent improvements over prior domain adaptation baselines across multiple NLP benchmarks covering sentiment analysis and natural language inference.
  • Demonstrates robustness under varying degrees of label-space mismatch, including open-set, partial, and fully open-partial scenarios.
  • The transferability scoring mechanism effectively separates known from unknown target samples, yielding high unknown-class detection accuracy.
  • Ablation studies confirm that each component (representation alignment, transferability scoring, unknown detection) contributes meaningfully to overall performance.

Why It Matters

Real-world NLP deployments rarely enjoy the luxury of matching label spaces between training and deployment domains. This work provides a principled framework that handles the most general form of domain shift, making NLP models more reliable when applied to new domains with potentially unseen categories. The approach is especially relevant for safety-critical applications where silently misclassifying out-of-domain inputs can be costly.
