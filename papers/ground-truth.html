Ground-Truth Labels Matter: A Deeper Look into Input-Label Demonstrations

EMNLP 2022
Kang Min Yoo, Junyeob Kim, Hyuhng Joon Kim, Hyunsoo Cho, Hwiyeol Jo, Sang-Woo Lee, Sang-goo Lee, Taeuk Kim

One-Line Summary

A deeper analysis showing that ground-truth labels in demonstrations play a more significant role in in-context learning than previously believed.

Background & Motivation

In-context learning (ICL) enables large language models to perform tasks by conditioning on a few input-label demonstrations without any parameter updates. A surprising prior finding (Min et al., 2022) suggested that the correctness of labels in demonstrations barely matters -- models performed nearly as well with random labels. This counterintuitive result challenged our understanding of how ICL works. This paper takes a deeper look, arguing that ground-truth labels do matter and that previous conclusions were artifacts of limited experimental settings.

Method

The paper conducts a comprehensive re-examination of the role of input-label mappings in ICL demonstrations:

  • Broader evaluation scope: Extends experiments beyond the narrow settings of prior work, testing across diverse model families, scales, and task types to reveal conditions where label correctness matters significantly
  • Noisy label analysis: Systematically varies the proportion of correct vs. random labels in demonstrations to measure the sensitivity of ICL to label quality
  • Disentangling contributing factors: Separates the effects of input distribution, label space, input-label mapping correctness, and formatting to isolate what each component contributes to ICL performance
  • Model scale investigation: Analyzes how model size affects sensitivity to ground-truth labels, revealing that larger models are better at leveraging correct demonstrations

Results

  • Ground-truth labels do matter: Contrary to prior claims, correct input-label mappings yield substantial performance gains, especially when evaluated across a broader set of conditions
  • Model scale amplifies label sensitivity: Larger models are significantly more sensitive to the correctness of demonstration labels, increasingly benefiting from ground-truth mappings
  • Task complexity plays a role: For more complex or fine-grained classification tasks, the gap between correct and random labels becomes more pronounced
  • Label noise degrades gracefully: Performance degrades proportionally as the fraction of incorrect labels increases, confirming that each correct demonstration contributes meaningful learning signal

Why It Matters

This work corrects an influential misconception in the ICL literature and restores the intuition that correct demonstrations teach models meaningful input-label mappings, not just formatting or label space priors. The findings have practical implications: when constructing demonstration sets for ICL, practitioners should prioritize label accuracy. More broadly, the paper deepens our understanding of the mechanism behind in-context learning and highlights the importance of rigorous, broad experimental evaluation before drawing sweeping conclusions.
