BlendX: Complex Multi-Intent Detection with Blended Patterns

LREC-COLING 2024
Yejin Yoon, Jungyeon Lee, Kangsan Kim, Chanhee Park, Taeuk Kim

One-Line Summary

A rigorous benchmark for multi-intent detection that goes beyond simple concatenation by blending single-intent utterances with diverse, complex linguistic patterns.

Background

Multi-intent detection (MID) addresses the realistic scenario where a single user utterance conveys multiple intents at once — studies show that over half of utterances in production dialogue systems contain more than one intent. However, dominant MID benchmarks such as MixATIS and MixSNIPS construct multi-intent examples using only four coordinating conjunctions ("and," "and then," "and also," and a comma). This overly simplistic construction allows models to exploit superficial concatenation cues rather than genuinely understanding semantics, inflating reported performance.

Method

BlendX constructs its benchmark along two orthogonal dimensions:

  • Complexity — Explicit vs. Implicit: Explicit concatenation employs diverse conjunctions (e.g., and, or, before, after, additionally). Implicit concatenation removes conjunctions entirely, using omissions, coreferences, and gerund phrases to create seamless blends that mirror natural speech.
  • Methodology — Naïve, Manual, Generative: Naïve replicates the original MixX recipe; Manual applies rule-based heuristics with varied connectors and implicit patterns; Generative leverages ChatGPT with SBERT-based similarity filtering (cosine ≥ 0.7) to produce natural multi-intent utterances.
  • Complexity Metrics: Three novel statistical measures — word-count reduction (omissions), conjunction absence, and pronoun introduction (coreferences) — quantify how much harder a dataset is than simple concatenation baselines.

Four widely used single-intent datasets (ATIS, SNIPS, Banking77, CLINC150) are extended into multi-intent versions with a 3:5:2 ratio of single / double / triple-intent utterances.

Results

  • Dramatic performance drops: Models trained on MixX and tested on BlendX suffer accuracy decreases of up to 40 percentage points (e.g., TFMN on Banking77: 76.61% → 37.31%), revealing that existing models heavily exploit superficial concatenation cues.
  • Models evaluated: TFMN (threshold-based), SLIM (binary classification), and ChatGPT (in-context learning). All struggle significantly on BlendX compared to MixX.
  • Ablation insights: Among the three construction methodologies, Manual concatenation (implicit patterns) is the most challenging, followed by Generative, then Naïve, confirming that implicit blending is the primary source of difficulty.
  • Training on BlendX helps but does not close the gap: Even when models are both trained and tested on BlendX, performance remains substantially below MixX-only evaluation, demonstrating BlendX's inherent complexity.

Why It Matters

BlendX exposes a critical blind spot in multi-intent detection research: published state-of-the-art results on MixATIS/MixSNIPS substantially overestimate real-world capability. By providing a more linguistically diverse and rigorous benchmark, BlendX redirects the field toward models that must truly understand compositional semantics rather than rely on shallow concatenation patterns. This has direct implications for building more robust task-oriented dialogue systems in production settings.
