Multilingual Chart-based Constituency Parse Extraction from Pre-trained Language Models

EMNLP 2021 Findings
Taeuk Kim, Bowen Li, Sang-goo Lee

One-Line Summary

A chart-based method for extracting multilingual constituency structures from pre-trained language models

Background

Prior work on extracting constituency parse trees from pre-trained language models has focused almost exclusively on English (primarily the Penn Treebank). However, multilingual PLMs such as mBERT and XLM-RoBERTa are trained on data from dozens of languages, raising the question of whether the syntactic structures they encode are language-specific or universal. Extending parse extraction to a multilingual setting is essential for understanding what structural knowledge these models learn and for enabling low-resource syntactic analysis in languages that lack large treebanks.

Method

  • Chart-based extraction: The method computes syntactic distance scores between adjacent words using the internal representations of a multilingual PLM. These distances are then used to fill a CYK-style chart, from which the highest-scoring binary constituency tree is decoded via dynamic programming.
  • Multilingual PLM probing: The approach is applied to multilingual models (mBERT, XLM-R) and evaluated on treebanks from multiple typologically diverse languages, including English, Chinese, German, French, Japanese, and others.
  • Cross-lingual transfer analysis: The paper investigates whether syntactic structures extracted from one language transfer to others, testing whether a single layer or distance metric works well across languages or whether language-specific tuning is required.

Results

  • Multilingual parse extraction works: Chart-based extraction from multilingual PLMs produces meaningful constituency trees across all tested languages, confirming that these models encode syntactic hierarchy beyond English.
  • Language-dependent optimal layers: The best-performing layer varies by language, suggesting that different layers specialize in different structural phenomena depending on the typological properties of the language.
  • Cross-lingual transferability: Settings optimized for one language often transfer reasonably to related languages, though performance drops for typologically distant pairs, indicating that structural encoding is partially universal but not fully language-agnostic.
  • Comparison with monolingual models: Multilingual models perform comparably to monolingual English models on English data while additionally supporting other languages, demonstrating that multilinguality does not significantly sacrifice English parsing quality.

Why It Matters

This work establishes that constituency parse extraction from PLMs is not an English-only phenomenon but generalizes across languages. It provides the first systematic multilingual evaluation of these methods, revealing both the promise (cross-lingual syntactic structure is present) and the limitations (optimal configurations are language-dependent). For low-resource languages without treebanks, this approach offers a viable path to approximate syntactic analysis using only a multilingual PLM, and the findings inform the design of future multilingual syntactic probing studies.
