Analysis of Multi-Source Language Training in Cross-Lingual Transfer

ACL 2024
Seong Hoon Lim, Taejun Yun, Jinhyeon Kim, Jihun Choi, Taeuk Kim

One-Line Summary

An analysis of training strategies using multi-source language data for cross-lingual transfer

Background

Cross-lingual transfer (XLT) leverages resource-rich source languages to improve NLP performance on low-resource target languages. While most prior work focuses on single-source language training (SSLT), typically using English, the research community lacks a comprehensive understanding of how multiple source languages interact when used together. Multilingual language models are known to separate language-specific and language-agnostic information, suggesting that training with diverse source languages might enhance language-independent features -- but which languages to combine, how many, and why certain combinations work better remain open questions.

Method

The authors systematically investigate Multi-Source Language Training (MSLT) by controlling total data volume across conditions (e.g., 1,000 English samples in SSLT vs. 500 English + 500 Spanish in MSLT) to ensure fair comparison.

1
MSLT vs. SSLT Comparison
Train XLM-R and BLOOM-7B on diverse tasks (WikiANN NER, XNLI, PAWS-X) with varying numbers of source languages (1-5) and visualize embeddings via t-SNE to assess language alignment
2
Language Selection Criteria
Systematically test multiple selection criteria: pretraining data size, vocabulary coverage, and linguistic diversity using Lang2Vec vectors (syntax, phonology, inventory, family, geometry)
3
Writing System & Diversity Analysis
Analyze how writing system diversity and typological distance among source languages correlate with transfer performance

Results

  • Optimal source count: Performance improves significantly from 1 to 3 source languages across all tasks, then plateaus -- 3 languages is the practical sweet spot
  • Combination matters: Up to 10-point performance gaps between optimal and poor language combinations; poor combinations can even underperform SSLT
  • Writing system diversity helps: Using 3 different writing systems yields 73.07% on WikiANN vs. 72.26% for same-script sources; XNLI shows a larger gap of 84.02% vs. 80.36%
  • Linguistic diversity outperforms statistical criteria: Lang2Vec-based selection consistently ranks in the top 1-4, while pretraining data size ranks 12-31
  • Top source languages: Chinese, Arabic, and German appear most frequently in top-5 combinations (17, 15, and 15 occurrences respectively)

Why It Matters

This work provides the first comprehensive analysis of multi-source language training for cross-lingual transfer, offering practical, actionable guidance for practitioners: choose 3 linguistically diverse source languages with different writing systems, selected via Lang2Vec-based criteria rather than data size heuristics. The findings generalize across architectures (XLM-R, BLOOM-7B), tasks (NER, inference, paraphrase detection), and training paradigms (fine-tuning, instruction-tuning), making MSLT a more predictable and effective strategy for low-resource language adaptation.
