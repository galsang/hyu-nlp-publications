A Syllable-based Technique for Word Embeddings of Korean Words

The 1st Workshop on Subword and Character Level Models in NLP (SCLeM 2017) at EMNLP 2017
Sanghyuk Choi, Taeuk Kim, Jinseok Seol, Sang-goo Lee

One-Line Summary

A CNN-based word representation model that uses Korean syllables as the basic embedding unit, producing morphologically meaningful word vectors that are robust to out-of-vocabulary words and capture the agglutinative structure of Korean.

Background & Motivation

Standard word embedding models like Word2Vec and GloVe treat words as atomic units, making them vulnerable to out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problems and unable to exploit subword information. These issues are magnified for agglutinative languages like Korean, where a single root morpheme can combine with ~60 different bound morphemes, causing vocabulary explosion.

Why Syllables? Korean characters have no inherent semantic meaning, while morpheme-based approaches require error-prone morphological analyzers. Korean syllables sit at the sweet spot: they carry semantic meaning (e.g., the syllables in "university" mean "big" + "study"), do not require preprocessing, and words sharing syllables often share semantics.

Proposed Method: Syllable-CNN

The model constructs word representations by composing trained syllable vectors through a convolutional neural network:

1
Syllable Embedding
Each Korean syllable is embedded into a d-dimensional vector space. A word's syllable vectors are concatenated in order to form the input representation.
2
Convolutional Layers
Multiple convolution filters with varying widths (1-4) are applied to capture local syllable patterns at different scales, followed by max pooling to extract the most salient features.
3
Skip-gram Training
The entire model (syllable embeddings + CNN) is trained jointly using the Skip-gram objective with negative sampling, predicting context words from the composed word representation.

Experimental Results

Evaluated on a Korean News corpus (2012-2014, ~2.7M tokens, 11K vocabulary, ~1K syllables) with vector dimension 320.

  • Word Similarity: Outperforms the Skip-gram baseline on the Korean-translated WS353-Similarity dataset (Pearson correlation: 0.634 vs. 0.583), showing that shared syllables help capture word similarity
  • OOV Robustness: Successfully handles newly coined words not in training data (e.g., "God Google", "Gal'Note") by composing syllable vectors, with nearest neighbors matching the original words they derive from
  • Morphological Awareness: PCA visualization shows the model forms discriminative parallel clusters between base words and their postposition-combined forms, while the baseline fails to capture this morphological structure
  • Efficient Coverage: Only ~1K syllables are needed to cover the entire vocabulary, compared to 11K word-level entries, making the approach highly parameter-efficient

Why It Matters

This work pioneered the use of syllables as the fundamental unit for Korean word representation, demonstrating that the unique syllabic structure of Hangul can be directly leveraged for NLP. The approach avoids the need for morphological analysis while naturally capturing Korean's agglutinative properties, providing a practical foundation for Korean NLP systems that must handle vocabulary variation and neologisms.
