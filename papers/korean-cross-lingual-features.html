Research on Features for Effective Cross-Lingual Transfer in Korean

The 35th Annual Conference on Human and Cognitive Language Technology (HCLT 2023)
Taejun Yun, Taeuk Kim

One-Line Summary

An empirical investigation into the linguistic and typological features that most strongly influence the success of cross-lingual transfer to Korean, providing guidance for selecting optimal source languages and configurations.

Background

Cross-lingual transfer using multilingual pretrained models has become a standard approach for building NLP systems in low-resource languages. However, transfer performance varies dramatically depending on the source language -- some languages transfer much better to Korean than others. Understanding which features drive this variation is crucial for designing effective cross-lingual systems. Previous work has examined cross-lingual transfer features broadly, but a focused study on Korean as the target language -- with its unique agglutinative morphology, SOV word order, and topic-prominent structure -- has been lacking.

Method

  • Feature analysis framework: A comprehensive set of linguistic features is examined, including typological features (word order, morphological type), lexical overlap, subword vocabulary overlap, and language family membership.
  • Controlled transfer experiments: Cross-lingual transfer experiments are conducted from multiple source languages to Korean under controlled settings, isolating the effect of each feature.
  • Multi-task evaluation: The analysis spans multiple NLP tasks (e.g., NER, POS tagging, text classification) to determine whether feature importance varies across tasks.
  • Correlation analysis: Statistical correlation between each feature and transfer performance is computed to identify the most predictive indicators.

Results

The study finds that subword vocabulary overlap and typological similarity (particularly word order and morphological type) are among the strongest predictors of transfer success to Korean. Surprisingly, language family membership alone is not as predictive as expected, suggesting that structural similarity matters more than genealogical relatedness. The relative importance of features also varies by task, with syntactic tasks being more sensitive to word order similarity.

Significance

This work provides practical guidance for practitioners building Korean NLP systems through cross-lingual transfer: rather than defaulting to English as the source language, one should consider typologically similar languages (e.g., Japanese, Turkish) that share structural features with Korean. The findings also contribute to the broader understanding of what makes cross-lingual transfer work, with implications beyond Korean.
