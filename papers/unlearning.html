Does Localization Inform Unlearning? A Rigorous Examination of Local Parameter Attribution for Knowledge Unlearning in Language Models

EMNLP 2025
Hwiyeong Lee, Uiji Hwang, Hyelim Lim, Taeuk Kim

One-Line Summary

We rigorously test the prevailing assumption that "identifying and modifying specific parameters can effectively remove unwanted knowledge" through controlled experiments, and demonstrate that this assumption does not hold.

Background & Motivation

"Unlearning" specific knowledge from LLMs is becoming increasingly important for privacy protection (GDPR "right to be forgotten"), harmful content removal, and copyright compliance.

The core assumption of existing approaches:

Assumption: Specific knowledge is stored "locally" in specific parameters of the model, so finding and modifying those parameters should be sufficient to remove the knowledge.

This study fundamentally re-examines the validity of this assumption.

Experimental Design

We design controlled experiments that eliminate confounders overlooked by prior work:

  • Parameter Locality Verification: Comparing modification of parameters presumed to "store knowledge" versus modification of other parameters
  • Diverse Localization Methods: Using multiple parameter attribution methods to verify consistency of results
  • General Capability Preservation: Evaluating whether overall language capabilities are maintained after knowledge removal

Key Findings

  • "Parameter Locality ≠ Effective Removal": Modifying parameters presumed to store the knowledge does not guarantee effective knowledge removal
  • Effectiveness of Non-Local Modification: Modifying "unrelated" parameters can achieve comparable or even better unlearning results — calling the locality assumption itself into question
  • Trade-off with General Capabilities: Localized approaches struggle to balance knowledge removal with preservation of general capabilities
  • Methodological Implications: Effective unlearning requires new approaches that go beyond parameter locality

Why It Matters

As regulations like the EU's GDPR "right to be forgotten" tighten, removing specific information from AI is a critical challenge both technically and legally. This study rigorously demonstrates the fundamental limitations of widely used localization-based unlearning methods, providing a foundation for the research community to explore more effective approaches. It delivers the important lesson that "knowing where to modify" does not mean "being able to effectively forget."
