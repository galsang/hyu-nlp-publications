ADVICE: Answer-Dependent Verbalized Confidence Estimation

ACL 2026
Ki Jung Seo, Sehun Lim, Taeuk Kim

One-Line Summary

We identify the "answer-independence" problem where LLMs express excessive confidence regardless of their actual answers, and propose ADVICE, an answer-grounded confidence estimation fine-tuning method.

Background & Motivation

Recent LLMs have gained the ability to verbalize their confidence levels in natural language, improving transparency. However, systematic overconfidence frequently occurs during this process. This study mathematically analyzes the root cause.

Key Finding: The core cause of overconfidence is "answer-independence," where models generate confidence scores independent of their actual answers. The Jensen-Shannon divergence between confidence distributions for correct and incorrect answers is nearly zero, proving that models output confidence without referencing the answer content.

Proposed Method: ADVICE Framework

ADVICE is a lightweight fine-tuning method that trains models to produce answer-grounded confidence. The key idea is to teach the model that confidence should differ between correct and incorrect answers.

1
Data Construction
Generate correct/incorrect answer pairs from 4,000 instances in TriviaQA
2
Contrastive Learning
Separate confidence distributions of correct and incorrect answers using 4 loss functions
3
Calibrated Inference
Generate calibrated confidence grounded in the answer

Roles of the 4 loss functions:

  • Language Modeling Loss: Preserves existing QA capabilities
  • Jensen-Shannon Divergence Loss: Distinguishes confidence distributions between correct and incorrect answers
  • Margin Loss: Ensures directional ordering so that correct answers have higher confidence than incorrect ones
  • Sum Loss: Imposes the constraint that μ_correct + μ_wrong = 1

Why It Matters

Knowing whether an AI is truly confident when it says "I'm sure" is critically important. In high-stakes domains such as healthcare, law, and finance, LLM overconfidence can lead to serious consequences. This study mathematically identifies the root cause of overconfidence (answer-independence) and presents a lightweight solution, laying the groundwork for building more trustworthy AI systems.
