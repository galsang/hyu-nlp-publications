SNU_IDS at SemEval-2018 Task 12: Sentence Encoder with Contextualized Vectors for Argument Reasoning Comprehension

International Workshop on Semantic Evaluation (SemEval 2018) at NAACL HLT 2018
Taeuk Kim, Jihun Choi, Sang-goo Lee

One-Line Summary

SECOVARC, a simple three-part neural architecture that uses contextualized word vectors from machine translation pre-training (CoVe) for argument reasoning comprehension, demonstrating the effectiveness of transfer learning for this challenging logical reasoning task.

Background & Motivation

The Argument Reasoning Comprehension task requires choosing the correct implicit warrant from two candidates, given a claim and a reason. This is challenging because it requires human-level reasoning to judge logical plausibility, often relying on common knowledge not present in the input.

Key Challenge: The training data is limited (~2K instances), making it difficult to train complex neural models from scratch. The paper hypothesizes that transfer learning from large-scale machine translation datasets can compensate for the data scarcity while keeping the model architecture simple.

Proposed Method: SECOVARC

SECOVARC (Sentence Encoder with COnextualized Vectors for Argument Reasoning Comprehension) is a three-layer architecture:

1
Encoding Layer
Uses CoVe (a two-layered Bi-LSTM pre-trained on large MT datasets) with GloVe embeddings to encode the claim, reason, and warrant into fixed-length sentence representations via max-pooling or last-state pooling
2
Localization Layer
Three separate fully-connected layers project each sentence representation into its own semantic space, maintaining role-specific information for claim, reason, and warrant
3
Output Layer
Combines five feature vectors -- claim, reason, warrant, element-wise difference, and element-wise product -- through logistic regression to produce a plausibility score between 0 and 1

Experimental Results

Evaluated on the SemEval-2018 Task 12 dataset (~2K crowdsourced instances). Results are reported as mean and standard deviation over 20 runs.

  • Best Test Performance: SECOVARC-max with heuristics achieves 59.2% test accuracy, outperforming all baselines and non-transferred models
  • Transfer Learning Works: SECOVARC consistently outperforms Bi-LSTM variants trained from scratch (BoW: 50.2%, Bi-LSTM-max: 54.3%), confirming the benefit of pre-trained contextualized vectors
  • Smaller Deviation: SECOVARC models show smaller standard deviations than non-transferred models, indicating more stable and successful training through transfer learning
  • Human Performance Gap: Human accuracy is 79.8% (or 90.9% with training in reasoning), showing significant room for model improvement

Why It Matters

This work is an early demonstration that transfer learning from machine translation can substantially improve performance on reasoning tasks with limited training data. The simple yet effective SECOVARC architecture showed that pre-trained contextualized representations (predating BERT/ELMo) are a key ingredient for natural language understanding tasks requiring logical reasoning.
