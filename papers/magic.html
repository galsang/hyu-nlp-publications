MAGIC: A Multi-Hop and Graph-Based Benchmark for Inter-Context Conflicts in Retrieval-Augmented Generation

EMNLP 2025 Findings
Jungyeon Lee, Kangmin Lee, Taeuk Kim

One-Line Summary

A benchmark that systematically generates knowledge conflicts between retrieved documents in RAG systems using knowledge graphs, and evaluates conflict detection and localization capabilities in multi-hop reasoning settings.

Background & Motivation

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique that retrieves external documents to enhance LLM responses. But what happens when retrieved documents contradict each other or conflict with the model's internal knowledge?

Limitations of Existing Benchmarks: Existing datasets (ECON, WikiContradict) consist of 78% single-hop conflicts, covering only simple contradictions. In real-world settings, complex contradictions that can only be discovered by connecting multiple documents are far more common.

Proposed Method: KG-Based Conflict Generation Framework

1
Subgraph Extraction
Filter 825 relations from Wikidata5M down to 46, and extract subgraphs via DFS traversal
2
Conflict Generation
Generate diverse conflicts using o3-mini with subgraph-level few-shot prompting, including human verification
3
Text Conversion
Convert to natural language with GPT-4o-mini; verify triplet coverage with Claude 3.5 Sonnet (accuracy: 95.21%)

Experimental Results

MAGIC consists of 1,080 instances and evaluates two capabilities: conflict identification (ID) and conflict localization (LOC).

BenchmarkID Score (%)LOC Score (%)
ECON (existing)74.7357.09
WikiContradict (existing)69.9355.74
MAGIC (proposed)64.5440.51
  • Best Performance: GPT-4o-mini achieves the highest with ID 83.61% and LOC 55%, yet still falls far short of human performance (ID 92.5%, LOC 83.3%)
  • Multi-hop vs Single-hop: Average ID score drops sharply from 70.91% to 46.13% for multi-hop conflicts
  • Difficulty of Localization: Pinpointing the exact location of conflicts is much harder than detecting their existence (LOC << ID)
  • Context Length Effect: Performance decreases as document length increases

Why It Matters

RAG is currently the most widely used LLM deployment pattern. If contradictions in retrieved results go undetected, incorrect information may be delivered to users. MAGIC leverages the interpretable structure of knowledge graphs to trace the causes and effects of conflicts, and is the first to systematically evaluate multi-hop conflicts, which are common in real-world settings.
